Rock features and rock mechanical properties

Rheology studies how matter flows –through other processes than just elastic deformation. Rheology is important for manufacturing and processing of food, but also for rocks and Earth Sciences, where it includes viscous (fluid), plastic, ductile flow and also brittle failure processes. Some of the important rock properties are its elastic stiffness (at least two parameters), its yield stress, flow law in case of ductile regime, tensile and compressive failure stresses, failure envelope and internal friction coefficient, how the response changes as a function of temperature, confining pressure and pore fluid pressure. Also very important are rock properties of fluid transport (permeability), heat transport (conductivity), magnetic susceptibility. Rocks generally are inhomogeneous (difference in properties) and heterogeneous (containing materials from different origins, inclusions such as dykes, xenoliths, and also structural peculiarities or defects). Because rocks have defects and discontinuities –they can be heavily jointed, fractured or faulted; boundaries separate different lithologies or mineral grains; dislocations disrupt the continuity of crystal lattices; they contain pores and cavities to variable extent. Small fractures and dislocations can act as flaws or imperfections, which considerably weaken the capacity of the rock to sustain stress without failing [Griffiths2017]. Inhomogeneities can also weaken the rock, if they have a different stiffness than the surrounding material, by creating stress concentrations.

3.3 Failure

There is more than one way to define the resilience of material to mechanical failure. One simple concept is that of compressive and tensile strength. One can define the limit value of stress supported by the rock in either cases. In the vast majority of cases, materials are weaker in tension than under compression. Importantly, although the stress applied to a solid may be well below the nominal strength, cavities or flows may concentrate locally stress inside it and induce its failure. Some type of cavities are extremely efficient at concentrating stress, and it is important to compute such concentration to understand and predict under what conditions materials will resist or will fail. This plays a role in many practical designs and problems: in the construction of tunnels or buildings where failure is avoided; in the use of hydrofracturing, where rupture is desirable; in the micromechanics of fracture, where even tiny cracks can weaken the material.
