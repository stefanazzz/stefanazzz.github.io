When deformation becomes too large (in excess of a few %) in rocks, elasticity breaks down and the deformation is accommodated through irreversible processes, of plastic, ductile or brittle nature. In the case of the brittle regime, the consequence is the formation of micro-cracks, cracks and fractures which coalesce to form small faults, and small faults which coalesce to form big faults, and the whole hyerarchy allows to accommodate permanent anelastic strain. Opening mode is observed at a small scale in cracks, or in granular materials (e.g. sand, or brecciated formations, or fault gouge), which accommodate dilation or compaction to a certain extent. The opposite (i.e. closing) can also take place through dissolution and evacuation of material, as this is the case for stylolithes. Transient fault opening can take place occasionally close to the surface, or during injection of molten magma (intrusion of dykes), or during fluid pressurization (induced seismicity and fracking). However, opening is relatively minor on faults. In the Earth the bulk of fault motion is achieved by shear, or frictional sliding of one bloc against the other. Faulting is a dissipative process: energy is dissipated into heat through friction and damage associated to fracturing; more energy is converted into waves during seismic motion (kinetic energy). These energy sinks are compensated by the release of elastic energy from the surrounding rocks. Therefore upon fault motion the net elastic energy in the surrounding volume drops. Because elastic energy changes are related to stress changes, fault motion has a relatively direct relation to the state of stress, in particular, to the direction of the major principal stress σ1 and the minor principal stress σ3. Fault motion can be either slow and aseismic, or in rapid jerks causing earthquakes.

5.2 Fault types


For simplicity, Andersonian faulting is often adopted, which assumes that one of the principal stresses is vertical. Gravity, an essential and pervasive force on Earth which is the source of lithostatic stress, defines the vertical axis. Therefore one can see that Anderson’s hypothesis has some fundamental logic and intuition to it, although it remains a first-order approximation. In this framework, the three types of faulting illustrated in Figure 5.2 are the relatted to a state of stress where:

  1. (a)

    major and minor principal stresses (namely σ1, σ3) are both horizontal, while intermediate ps σ2 is vertical;

  2. (b)

    major principal stress is vertical, while σ3 is horizontal;

  3. (c)

    major principal stress is horizontal, while σ3 is vertical.

When reducing the investigation to two dimensions (for example, in a vertical section), one often discards the intermediate stress, and conducts the analysis in terms of two principal stresses only. Therefore it is customary to use σ1 referring to the major principal stress and σ2 to the minor one. The analysis of chapter 1 around equations 1.27 falls back onto such two dimensional simplification.

5.3 Friction and Mohr-Coulomb

Sliding, or failure, occurs when frictional force is overcome. The most simple failure criterion known as Mohr-Coulomb, predicts a linear dependence of failure shear stress τ on the normal stress σ:

τ=μ⁢σ+C (5.1)

where μ is friction and C is cohesion.


5.4 Pore fluid pressure and effective stress

Small cavities in rocks as pores and cracks are very often filled with fluids such as water with various minerals in solution (and occasionally petrol or methane, although less and less so since the 19th century). Water in the Earth crust has both chemical and mechanical effect. We will shortly introduce the mechanical effect of water on the effective or apparent strength of the material. In Figure 5.4 a crack or fault is represented where the two faces are not matching perfectly. The resulting interstices are filled with fluids (eventually the fluids may precipitate new mineralisation creating solid veins of material which can be similar or very different from the host rock).


The fluid pressure will act against on the surface of the solid and contribute to the the stress perpendicular to the surface (normal stress), but not to the stress parallel to the surface (shear stress) because fulids cannot support shear elastically. (Fluids may sustain viscous shear stress if deforming at a substantial shear rate, and this can happen during an earthquake, but we do not consider this situation in this chapter). For a “dry” medium, we have the force equilibrium: F=σc⁢Ac.
For a “wet” medium, we have: F=σc⁢Ac+Pf⁢(A-Ac).
Dividing by A to obtain the average normal stress σ=F/A we write:

σ=FA=σc⁢AcA+Pf⁢A-AcA.

Now the average effective stress supported through the grain contacts (or asperities) is only σe=σc⁢Ac/A, so we can write:

σe=σ-P⁢A-AcA=σ-α⁢Pf (5.2)

where α is known as the Biot coefficient. For want of a more precise measurement, it is often assumed that Ac≪A such that α=A-AcA≈1 and the effective stress is:

σe=σ-Pf (5.3)

Unless shear rate and fluid viscosity are high, shear is only supported by the contacting area Ac, and not by the fluid, Therefore the frictional resistance is reduced because the effective normal stress is reduced, and Equation 5.1 should be written instead:

τ=μ⁢σe+C=μ⁢(σ-α⁢Pf)+C. (5.4)

5.5 Static and sliding friction

The notion of static friction applies when two surfaces are in contact without sliding, i.e., in an object (a rock) which is at rest. Static friction acts to precisely cancel out the external force that would otherwise cause slipping. When the maximum friction μs or static friction coefficient is reached, slip will start across the contact surface, at which point the surface will go from a state of static friction to a state of sliding friction. Amonton’s second principle states that sliding will occur only if shear stress is equal or greater than the normal stress σ times the static friction coefficient μs, and this corresponds to the Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion (which we have already seen before in eq. 5.4 for example), however in the specific case of Amonton the cohesion C is zero:

τc=μs⁢σ (critical shear stress at failure)τ≤μs⁢σ (shear stress before failure) (5.5)

When sliding, friction does not compensate exactly the applied force any more. However, there is still a level resistance to sliding, which is manifest as a force which is in the opposite direction of the sliding. The amount of resisistance to sliding, or sliding friction, is described by a new coefficient which has usually a lower value than the static friction coefficient. The sliding coefficient is usually variable, and can be a function of the slip U, or the slip velocity V, or some state variable which represents the evolving state of the surface, or a combination of all of the above. In many cases, sliding friction is still proportional to normal stress, and we can write:

τ=μd⁢(U,V,…)⁢σ (sliding shear stress) (5.6)

where μd⁢(U,V,…) is now the dynamic or sliding friction coefficient. The value and evolution of μd, and what controls it, is a very complex matter and topic of advanced research. In engineering, friction study is called tribology, and one main concern is how to effectively reduce friction or lubricate surfaces for industrial applications. On a lubricated surface, friction depends weakly on normal stress, however resistance due viscous shear of a fluid layer at the sliding interface can become important. Indeed lubrication relies on the presence of a third body between the two sliding surfaces. In Earth sciences, research on friction in rocks is important for understanding faults, earthquake sources and landslides.

5.5.1 Slip-weakening friction

There are many models to describe sliding friction. The most simple model uses just a constant sliding friction coefficient μd= Const, which is less that the static one, i.e. μd<μs. However, it is not likely that friction drops very suddenly from μs to μd. Therefore a slightly mode complex model is introduced, called slip-weakening friction. When sliding starts, the friction drops gradually, and the more the slip advances, the weaker it becomes, until it reaches a minimum value. This can be expressed as:

μd=μm⁢i⁢n+(μs-μm⁢i⁢n)⁢(1-UDc) for ⁢U<Dcμd=μm⁢i⁢n for ⁢U≥Dc (5.7)

5.5.2 Rate-and-state friction

One important contribution to the field of friction has been made by experimentalists studying rock sliding friction to understand earthquake faults. They defined a mathematical model to represent how friction evolves. They called it rate-and-state friction. In this model, friction is controlled by two variables: a state variable θ and velocity of slip V, and three fixed parameters: a,b, and Dc which can be described by a system of two coupled equations:

μ=μ0+a⁢log⁡VV0+b⁢log⁡θ⁢V0Dc(I)∂t⁡θ=1-V⁢θDc(II) (5.8)

where V0 is a reference sliding velocity, Dc is a fixed parameter with units of distance, V is sliding velocity, and θ is a state variable (somehow encapsulating the present state of the surface) with units of time; μ0 is a constant. Equation (II) is called the evolution law, it represents how the surface evolves with slip. There are several possible forms for (II), this is just one model.

The area below the shear stress curve is in units of MPa×m. If we replace MPa by N m-2 (force per unit area) we obtain for the area units of N m-1. Given that energy (work) is normally in N m, and that the graphic represents what takes place on a unit surface of the fault, we can say that the area’s dimensions could represent an energy density, or energy per unit fault surface. (3) Friction is a dissipative process. Dissipation generally transforms energy into heat. So the energy goes into heat, which means that the temperature rises, and then gradually diffuses away from the fault (or, in some cases, causes melting). (4) Same units as G, or fracture energy. Considering the fact that fracture energy tends to resist rupture propagation, and friction tends to resist slip, both are acting against the progress of rupture. In addition, the units are the same. So we may interpret the area under the shear stress curve as an equivalent of fracture energy. This concept of frictional-related fracture energy is complementary to the concept of fault-damage zone fracture energy.  
Answer to Exercise 5.28 – Exploring rate-and-state friction
(1)

if ∂t⁡θ=1-V⁢θDc=0then θ=Dc/V

replacing θ in equation (I) we obtain

μs⁢s=μ0+a⁢log⁡VV0+b⁢log⁡V0V

but we note that log⁡V0V=-log⁡VV0 so we can group the terms accordingly:

μs⁢s=μ0+(a-b)⁢log⁡VV0

(2) (a-b)>0 will result in rate-hardening (higher friction at higher velocity) while (a-b)<0 will result in the opposite (rate-weakening). (3) If the friction drops when the fault slides faster, then the fault is going to slide even faster, as the brakes are off. The faster it slides, the lower the friction, and so positive feedback mechanism will make it more likely that the fault becomes unstable. Instead, if the friction increases when the fault tends to accelerate, it will act as a negative feedback and the fault will be stable, in the sense that it will not generate seismic earthquakes, because they require a very high slip velocity (of the order of 1 m/s), or at least slip acceleration, even in the case of so-called slow earthquakes. (4) If the fault stops and V=0 the rate-and-state law mathematical model does not work any more. A zero inside a log will give -∞ which is clearly not possible. So the law certainly stops working for stuck faults. Because the log dependence is rather weak at high velocity, the friction will also drop very modestly according to rate-and-state models, even during fast, earthquake slip. But dramatic weakening is observed at fast slip, so that is another limitation of r&s.
