2.1 Hooke’s law for elongation; Young’s modulus


If the bar is constituted of elastic material, and its section area is A , the deformation will produce a proportional reaction force F such that:

F∝Δ⁢lF∝Δ⁢llF=E⁢A⁢Δ⁢ll (2.1)

where E is known as Young’s modulus, and has dimensions of N m2 or Pa (Pascals). We can obtain a stress by taking the ratio F/A such that:

FA=E×Δ⁢ll=σx⁢x, (2.2)
Stress=(Young’s modulus)×(Elongation)

where ℓ lies in the x direction.

2.2 Poisson effect; Poisson ratio

As shown in Figure 3.7 There will also be a lateral shrinkage, by an amount:

Δ⁢ww=Δ⁢hh=-ν⁢Δ⁢ll (2.3)
Contraction=-(Poisson ratio)×(Elongation)

where ν is the dimensionless Poisson ratio.


Δ⁢dd=-ν×Δ⁢llContraction=(Poisson ratio)×(Elongation) (2.4)

So far we have defined 2 prameters, E and ν, to describe the longitudinal and lateral deformation under unixial force. Can the same two parameters be used to describe other types of distortion?

2.3 Uniform compression; bulk modulus

Hydrostatic pressure of a liquid is automatically the same in all directions. Within a solid it is traction that acts rather than pressure, and it is not necessarily isotropic. However, we can imagine a situation where

σx⁢x=σy⁢y=σz⁢z=p. (2.5)

For example, if a solid bloc is immersed in water at some depth.


In this case the relative change in size would be the same for all sides. To solve for the strain in direction ℓ, let’s add the pressure p in one direction at the time. By principle of linear superposition, the final deformation will be the same as adding pressure on all three directions at once. We start by adding p in direction ℓ and using equation 2.2 with σx⁢x=p we get the strain:

Δ⁢ℓ1ℓ=-pE (2.6)

This will have effects on the w and h edges, and we have reached a new equilibrium shape for the object. Starting from this new situation, we now apply a pressure p to the top: h will shrink to a new equilibrium position by an amount

Δ⁢hh=-pE (2.7)

This new deformation, in virtue of the Poisson effect, will induce a rebound of ℓ by an amount

Δ⁢ℓ2ℓ=ν⁢pE. (2.8)

Finally we add p in the w direction, which will induce a further deformation on ℓ by an amount

Δ⁢ℓ3ℓ=ν⁢pE. (2.9)

At this point the total strain in ℓ is such that:

Δ⁢ℓℓ=Δ⁢ℓ1ℓ+Δ⁢ℓ2ℓ+Δ⁢ℓ3ℓ=PE⁢(1-2⁢ν). (2.10)

Although in our thought experiment we have proceeded in three steps, the final situation is equilibrium due to the same sum of forces, so it does not matter whether we apply them in a different sequence, or all together at once when the object is immersed in the water. Using this result, solve exercise 2.8 to show that the relative change of volume V of the bloc under isotropic stress (or pressure), if the deformation is infinitesimal, is such that:

p=Δ⁢VV⁢E3⁢(1-2⁢ν). (2.11)

We define

K=E3⁢(1-2⁢ν) (2.12)

as the Bulk modulus. This modulus tells us how much pressure is necessary to reduce the volume of an object by a given amount. It has the same dimensions as the Young modulus (Pa or N/m2)

Finally, we can combine the two parameters E,ν to relate the shear stress to the shear deformation:


In this case we can define the shear modulus G:

G=E2⁢(1+ν)⁢shear⁢modulus (2.13)

such that

τ=2⁢G⁢εx⁢y=G⁢εx⁢y+G⁢εy⁢x. (2.14)

2.5 Shear and stress deviator

In the previous section we have shown how to construct shear force from compressive and tensile forces alone. Let’s revisit those results assuming that the edges of the larger square in Figure 2.5 are in in directions (x,y). Then σx⁢x=Fℓ⁢2 and σy⁢y=-Fℓ⁢2. If the edges of the smaller square are in directions (x′,y′), then σx′⁢y′=σy′⁢x′=Fℓ⁢2. The stress tensor in (x,y) has only diagonal elements which are not zero:

𝝈=(σx⁢x00σy⁢y)=1ℓ⁢2⁢(F00-F) (2.20)

To go from (x,y) to (x′⁢y′) we perform a rotation of π/2. Now recalling the results of Equation 1.22 and Figure 1.3, the stress tensor in the new referential (x′,y′) has only off-diagonal non-zero elements:

𝝈′=(0τx′⁢y′τy′⁢x′0)=1ℓ⁢2⁢(0FF0) (2.21)

However, had we taken equal compressive forces in the initial tensor, this would have resulted in:

𝝈=(F00F)⁢rot.⟶⁢𝝈′=(F00F) (2.22)

i.e., rotation would produce zero shear stress, and conserve the diagonal elements! You can try to compute the rotation result for yourselves, and find that this last result would hold for any angle of rotation. Therefore, the origin of shear stress is the difference between the two principal stresses. Such difference is also called the deviatoric stress or stress deviator. For a general state of stress, we obtain the deviator apparent by removing the trace divided by two, such that:

𝝈=(σx⁢x00σy⁢y)=(σx⁢x-σy⁢y200σy⁢y-σx⁢x2)+(1/2⁢t⁢r⁢(𝝈)001/2⁢t⁢r⁢(𝝈))DEVIATOR+HYDROSTATIC PART (2.23)

upon a rotation of θ we will obtain:

𝝈′=(σx′⁢x′τx′⁢y′τy′⁢x′σy′⁢y′)=(σx⁢x-σy⁢y2⁢cos⁡2⁢θσx⁢x-σy⁢y2⁢sin⁡2⁢θσx⁢x-σy⁢y2⁢sin⁡2⁢θσy⁢y-σx⁢x2⁢cos⁡2⁢θ)+(1/2⁢t⁢r⁢(𝝈)001/2⁢t⁢r⁢(𝝈)) (2.24)

where tr⁢(𝝈)=σx⁢x+σy⁢y, i.e., the first invariant I1 of the stress tensor defined in Equation 1.13. What is the meaning of Equation 2.24? It shows that the shear stress does not depend on the absolute value of stress, and it is independent on the trace of the stress. Shear stress only depends on the difference between compressive stresses (σx⁢x-σy⁢y) along perpendicular directions, or stress deviator. A state of isotropic compressive stress (similar to pressure in a fluid) will not produce any shear stress. This can also be seen from Equation 1.27, where τ is zero whenever σ1=σ2.

2.6 A note on strain energy

Strain energy is the energy stored by a body undergoing deformation. It equates to the amount of work done by forces (or stresses) onto the body during the deformation of the body. For simplicity, I will write strain and stress in the form of a single scalar quantities ε and σ. Take for example a case of uniaxial strain in direction x, where σ≡σx⁢x and ε≡εx⁢x. The stress and strain are related through Hooke’s law σ=E⁢ε where E is Young modulus as of Equation 2.2. At each point inside a solid body (a rock for example) we can write an elementary strain energy density d⁢ρE, produced by an elementary strain d⁢ε:

d⁢ρE=σ⁢(ε)⁢d⁢εwork density for elementary strain ⁢d⁢ε (2.25)

We can obtain the energy density ρE resulting from a finite strain ε by integration:

ρE=∫0εσ⁢(ε)⁢𝑑εenergy densityρE=∫0εE⁢ε⁢𝑑ερE=12⁢E⁢ϵ2=12⁢1E⁢σ2 (2.26)

Note that the energy density is proportional to the square of the deformation, and the square of the stress; this is important because the same amount of deformation can preduce different energies depending on the initial condition of the solid (if it is already loaded with some stress, the work will be greater). Finally, if the solid which is under study has a volume V, to obtain the net strain energy U we need to integrate over the volume,

U=∫VρE⁢(𝐱)⁢𝑑𝐱total energy in volume ⁢V (2.27)

which in case of homogeneous deformation will result in:

U=ρE×Vif ⁢ρ⁢ is homogeneous (2.28)

2.7 General isotropic elastic relations

Dealing with elastic stress and strain tensors, in the most general situation we would need to relate each component of the stress:

σi⁢j=Ci⁢j⁢k⁢l⁢εk⁢l (2.29)

where Ci⁢j⁢k⁢l is the elastic parameter tensor. We have seen in Equation 1.12 that Ci⁢j⁢k⁢l, because it relates two second rank tensors, should be a fourth order tensor with up to 81 components. However it turns out that there are symmetries in the C tensor which allow to reduce the number of its independent components down to 21. Still, if the solid is isotropic, the number of independent parameters is reduced to….. 2 only! In this case we can write:

Ci⁢j⁢k⁢l=λ⁢δi⁢j⁢δk⁢l+G⁢(δi⁢k⁢δj⁢l+δi⁢l⁢δj⁢k) (2.30)

using these two parameters λ and G which are simply a combination of the two same old parameters that we already met (Young and Poisson):

λ=E⁢ν(1+ν)⁢(1-2⁢ν)G=E⁢12+2⁢ν (2.31)

and the definition of the Kroneker δi⁢j such that

δi⁢j=0   if   i≠jδi⁢j=1   if   i=j (2.32)

It follows that stress can be related to strain by using

σi⁢j=λ⁢δi⁢j⁢εk⁢k+G⁢(δi⁢k⁢δj⁢l+δi⁢l⁢δj⁢k)⁢εk⁢lorσ¯=λ⁢trace⁢(ε¯)⁢𝐈+2⁢G⁢ε¯ (2.33)

and by expanding all possible individual index values we get:

σx⁢x=λ⁢(εx⁢x+εy⁢y+εz⁢z)+2⁢G⁢εx⁢xσy⁢y=λ⁢(εx⁢x+εy⁢y+εz⁢z)+2⁢G⁢εy⁢yσz⁢z=λ⁢(εx⁢x+εy⁢y+εz⁢z)+2⁢G⁢εz⁢zσx⁢y=2⁢G⁢εx⁢y;σx⁢z=2⁢G⁢εx⁢z;σy⁢z=2⁢G⁢εy⁢z; (2.34)

Alternatively, it is possible to write the inverse relation as

εi⁢j=1E⁢(σi⁢j-ν⁢[σk⁢k⁢δi⁢j-σi⁢j])or𝜺=1E⁢(𝝈-ν⁢[tr⁢(𝝈)⁢𝐈-𝝈]) (2.35)

where

𝐈=(100010001). (2.36)

and

ε11 =1E⁢[σ11-ν⁢(σ22+σ33)] (2.37)
ε22 =1E⁢[σ22-ν⁢(σ11+σ33)] (2.38)
ε33 =1E⁢[σ33-ν⁢(σ11+σ22)] (2.39)
ε12 =1G⁢σ12;ε13=1G⁢σ13;ε23=1G⁢σ23 (2.40)

where the subscripts 1,2,3 are equivalent to x,y,z. You can replace ν and E by their corresponding values in terms of λ and G, if you wish.

Answer to Exercise 2.11   For each successive stretching, we have to work against an increased initial tension of the rubber band. Therefore the work will be larger for step II than for step I, and for step III than for step II, even if the amount of additional stretching is the same. The first stretching results in total elongation Δ⁢ℓℓ|I=2ℓ (for any value of initial length ℓ in cm); after the second and third stretching, the total elongation is Δ⁢ℓℓ|I⁢I=4ℓ and Δ⁢ℓℓ|I⁢I⁢I=6ℓ, respectively. The corresponding work (and the resulting energy stored in the rubber band) is E2⁢(Δ⁢ℓℓ)2=Δ⁢ℓ2⁢E2⁢ℓ resuting in 4, 16 and 36 ×E2⁢ℓ for cases I, II and III. In short, the stored energy is the square of the total deformation. The change in energy between case II and III is 9/4 times as much as the change in energy between I and II, although the added stretching is exactly the same. If an episode of deformation of the lithosphere is measured, it is not trivial to derive what the corresponding internal change of energy is. Indeed, the initial state of stress before the episode would need to be known. This would require that either all the previous deformation episodes are known (so the total cumulative strain is known), or that we can measure the absolute state of stress inside the Earth (which in many cases is impossible).

(b) Rupture will occur as soon as σ1-σ2 reaches the minimum possible value for the failure criterion to be met. The question is what is the angle θ where such minimum value is met? Therefore we need to find the minimum value of σ1-σ2 as a function of θ. Noting that here

σ2=P.

We can start working on equation (2.45):

σ1=μ⁢P⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+P⁢sin⁡2⁢θ+μ⁢P+2⁢τ0μ⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+sin⁡2⁢θ-μσ1⁢(μ⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+sin⁡2⁢θ-μ)=μ⁢P⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+P⁢sin⁡2⁢θ+μ⁢P+2⁢τ0σ1⁢(μ⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+sin⁡2⁢θ+μ)-2⁢μ⁢σ1=μ⁢P⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+P⁢sin⁡2⁢θ+μ⁢P+2⁢τ0σ1⁢(μ⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+sin⁡2⁢θ+μ)-2⁢μ⁢σ1=P⁢(μ⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+sin⁡2⁢θ+μ)+2⁢τ0(σ1-P)⁢(μ⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+sin⁡2⁢θ+μ)=2⁢τ0+2⁢μ⁢σ1

σ1-P=2⁢(μ⁢σ1+τ0)μ⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+sin⁡2⁢θ+μ

and differentiate it with respect to θ, to obtain:

(f⁢(g⁢(θ)))′=g′⁢(θ)⁢f′⁢(g⁢(θ))
