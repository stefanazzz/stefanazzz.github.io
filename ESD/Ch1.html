1.1 Body and surface forces on a solid

Body forces are forces acting on a volume (they are also called volume forces).One example is gravity, which attracts each point inside a volume with a force density 𝐟=ρ×𝐠 (N m-3), where ρ is the mass density) and results in a net force 𝐅=V×ρ×𝐠⁢ (N) for an object of volume V. Surface forces however act on a surface, for example, a pressurized gas in a piston will exert a pressure P (N m-2) against the piston head; if the piston axis direction is parallel to the unit vector 𝐧 the force surface density will be 𝐟=P×𝐧 (N m-2) and the net force 𝐅=A×P×𝐧⁢ (N) if A is the area of the piston head. Pressure, a force per unit surface, is a scalar, and pressure is the same in every direction. However the net force that pressure exerts on a given surface is perpendicular to such surface (e.g. along surface normal vector 𝐧, like for the piston head). In a solid there is a quantity similar to pressure, also a surface force, however it is not a scalar but a vector. Unlikely pressure, it can change with direction, and it does not necessarily act perpendicular to the surface. It is called traction. The definition of traction across a surface of normal vector 𝐧 is

Ti(𝐧)=limΔ⁢S→0⁡Δ⁢FiΔ⁢S, (1.1)

where Ti is the it⁢h component of the traction across the surface, Fi is the it⁢h component of the force across the surface, and Δ⁢S is the area of the surface. An illustration of the traction vector is found in Figure 1.1. To reflect on the difference between body forces and surface forces, discuss Exercise 1.1.


Traction vectors acting on opposite sides of the same surface are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction, i.e.,

-𝐓(𝐧)=𝐓(-𝐧). (1.2)

This fundamental lemma is equivalent to Newton’s third law of motion of action and reaction.

1.3 The stress tensor

In fact, because in a volume we can define three mutually perpendicular surfaces, we need at least three traction vectors to define the internal state of stress. From the three traction vectors on mutually perpendicular surfaces, we can then compute traction on any surface by some type of rotation. It three tractions are necessary, this would in principle result in 3×3=9 distinct components; we can organise these nine components them in the form of a tensor: the stress tensor. For example, let 𝐓(𝐢)=(Tx(𝐢),Ty(𝐢),Tz(𝐢)) be the traction vector acting on the (y,z) plane, whose normal vector is 𝐢. Similarly, 𝐓(𝐣) acts on the (x,z) plane, whose normal vector is 𝐣, and 𝐓(𝐤) acts on the (x,y) plane, whose normal vector is 𝐤. Tractions acting on each of the three surfaces can then be organised in three lines and three columns:

(Tx(𝐢)Ty(𝐢)Tz(𝐢)Tx(𝐣)Ty(𝐣)Tz(𝐣)Tx(𝐤)Ty(𝐤)Tz(𝐤)) (1.3)

This notation however is quite unusual, and we prefer to use the standard one, that is:

𝝈=(σx⁢xσx⁢yσx⁢zσy⁢xσy⁢yσy⁢zσz⁢xσz⁢yσz⁢z) (1.4)

Equation 1.4 represents the full stress tensor, where each element equates to the corresponding traction component in Equation 1.3. Therefore, the significance of the two subscripts is as follows: The first subscript indicates the perpendicular to the surface on which the stress component is applied. The second one indicates the direction in which the stress is acting. This is new text

Try now to draw an equivalent 3D cube (see Exercise 1.3). We have seen how to build the stress tensor from the tractions. To do the inverse operation, there is a way to calculate the traction acting on any surface of normal vector 𝐧, from the stress tensor components, and that is:

Ti(𝐧)=σi⁢j⁢nj (1.5)

where the summation on the index j is implicit, therefore we can write the x component of 𝐓(𝐧) as

Tx(𝐧)=σx⁢x⁢nx+σx⁢y⁢ny+σx⁢z⁢nz (1.6)

This mathematical operation, taking the product of a tensor and a vector, allows to go from a tensor to vector. Can you write Ty(𝐧) and Tz(𝐧)? Can you write Tx(𝐢) where 𝐢 is the normal vector pointing in direction x?

1.4 Scalar, vector, tensor

In the above sections we have introduced scalar, vector and tensor quantities. Would you be able to tell which they are, and give additional examples of vectors and scalar quantities in Physics or Earth Sciences? Would you be able to give examples of tensors, and how they differ from a scalar and vector? A scalar is a quantity having only magnitude, no direction. It is determined by a single numerical value. Examples of scalars are temperature (oC or oK), pressure (Pa or N m-2), mass density (kg m-3), diffusivity (thermal, hydraulic, …m2 s). Here are a few indicative (and potentially useful to keep in mind) orders of mangitude for scalar quantities encountered in Earth and Planetary Sciences: Temperatures:

  • 0o⁢C (Earth surface)

  • 6000o⁢C (Earth inner core)

  • 6000o⁢C (surface of the Sun)

Pressures on Earth:

  • ρ⁢g⁢h≈107⁢ Pa at 1 km depth in the Ocean

  • 108⁢ Pa at 10 km depth in the Ocean

  • surface 1 atm = 1 bar = 105 Pa

  • crust < 0-1 GPa (technically, it’s lithostatic load, not pressure…)

  • mantle 1-140 GPa

  • inner core 330-360 GPa

remember the rule ρ⁢g⁢h where ρ⁢g⁢h≈107→108⁢ Pa (where ρ is mass density of water, g is gravity, h is depth. A vector has both magnitude and direction, and requires three values instead of one:

𝐕=(Vx,Vy,Vz) (1.7)

If the frame of reference (x,y,z) is changed to (x′,y′,z′) by a rotation, then the components of the same vector in the new frame of reference are different:

𝐕=(Vx′,Vy′,Vz′);Vx′≠Vx, etc.  (1.8)

but the magnitude of the vector Vx′2,Vy′2,Vz′2=Vx2,Vy2,Vz2 remains the same; it is invariant. A tensor is an object analogous a vector: it is represented by an array of components that are functions of the coordinates of a space. However a tensor is more general, it maps in a multi-linear manner geometric vectors, scalars, or other tensors. To make this a little less murky, imagine you have two vectors:

𝐏=(Px,Py,Pz)    𝐐=(Qx,Qy,Qz) (1.9)

which can be related to one anther by a linear combination of their components. We need to relate each component of vector 𝐏 to all the components of 𝐐, i.e.,this kind of operation:

Px=αx⁢x⁢Qx+αx⁢y⁢Qy+αx⁢z⁢QzPy=αy⁢x⁢Qx+…Pz=… (1.10)

where αx⁢x relates Px to Qx, αx⁢y relates Px to Qy, and so on. We need nine different α.., and each value has two subscripts indicating which components of the vectors it is combining. This makes 𝜶 a tensor or rank 2, and we can write the operation computing 𝐏 as:

𝐏=(PxPyPz)=(αx⁢xαx⁢yαx⁢zαy⁢xαy⁢yαy⁢zαz⁢xαz⁢yαz⁢z)⋅(QxQyQz)=𝜶⋅𝐐 (1.11)

The boldface notation usually indicates that we’re looking at a vector or a tensor, while the normal face indicates a scalar quantity. Individual components of vectors or tensors are not bold. So here we start to see the structure of scalars, vectors and tensors, that can be summed up in the array below:

single componenta scalar: un/aua vector :𝒖⁢ or ⁢u→ui(ux,uy,uz)2nd rank tensor: 𝝉τi⁢j(τx⁢xτx⁢yτx⁢zτy⁢xτy⁢yτy⁢zτz⁢xτz⁢yτz⁢z)

By the same way, two second rang tensors can be linearly related by using a fourth rank tensor:

σx⁢x=Cx⁢x⁢x⁢x⁢εx⁢x+Cx⁢x⁢y⁢y⁢εy⁢y+… (1.12)

Can you guess how many C…. components we need in total? As you might have guessed, scalars are actually tensors, too, it’s just that they are rank zero tensors, while vectors are tensors of rank one (which is why they have a single subscript)! So it turns out that you’ve been using tensors all your life, you can relax…

1.6 Stress rotation

Consider a surface which is rotated with respect to the referential, such that its normal vector 𝐧 is oriented at an angle α with respect to the x axis. We therefore have 𝐧=(nx,ny)=(cos⁡α,sin⁡α) (for simplicity we only consider the 2D case in the (x,y) plane). Given Equation 1.5 we can compute the component of tractions in direction x and y for a surface of normal 𝐧:

Tx(𝐧)=σx⁢x⁢cos⁡α+τx⁢y⁢sin⁡αTy(𝐧)=τy⁢x⁢cos⁡α+σy⁢y⁢sin⁡α (1.15)

then we need to project project T(𝐧) onto the surface-perpendicular and surface-parallel directions of surface 𝐧 to obtain the tractions in the referential of the rotated surface:

T⟂(𝐧)=Tx(𝐧)⁢cos⁡α+Ty(𝐧)⁢sin⁡αT//(𝐧)=-Tx(𝐧)⁢sin⁡α+Ty(𝐧)⁢cos⁡α (1.16)

and defining σ=T⟂(𝐧) and τ=T//(𝐧) this expands into:

σ=(σx⁢x⁢cos⁡α+τx⁢y⁢sin⁡α)⁢cos⁡α+(τy⁢x⁢cos⁡α+σy⁢y⁢sin⁡α)⁢sin⁡ατ=-(σx⁢x⁢cos⁡α+τx⁢y⁢sin⁡α)⁢sin⁡α+(τy⁢x⁢cos⁡α+σy⁢y⁢sin⁡α)⁢cos⁡α. (1.17)

Finally noting that

sin⁡α⁢cos⁡α=12⁢sin⁡2⁢αcos2⁡α-sin2⁡α=cos⁡2⁢α (1.18)

we obtain after some algebra that:

σ=12⁢(σx⁢x+σy⁢y)+12⁢(σx⁢x-σy⁢y)⁢cos⁡2⁢α+τx⁢y⁢sin⁡2⁢ατ=12⁢(σy⁢y-σx⁢x)⁢sin⁡2⁢α+τx⁢y⁢cos⁡2⁢α (1.19)

which are the normal stress and the shear stress on our rotated surface. We Note that the shear stress τ in 1.19 can vanish provided that:

tan⁡2⁢α0=2⁢τx⁢yσy⁢y-σx⁢x (1.20)

Therefore the shear stress vanishes at the values of α such that:

α0={arctan⁡2⁢τx⁢yσy⁢y-σx⁢xarctan⁡2⁢τx⁢yσy⁢y-σx⁢x+π/2arctan⁡2⁢τx⁢yσy⁢y-σx⁢x+πarctan⁡2⁢τx⁢yσy⁢y-σx⁢x+3⁢π/2 (1.21)

each time this happens we only have σ, the normal stress, acting on the rotated surface. Assuming that the referential is chisen such that x′ is aligned with thi first value of α in 1.21, we may define stresses σx′⁢x′=σ1 and σy′⁢y′=σ2 such that:

σ=12⁢(σ1+σ2)+12⁢(σ1-σ2)⁢cos⁡2⁢ατ=-12⁢(σ1-σ2)⁢sin⁡2⁢α (1.22)

σ1 and σ2 are known as the principal stresses. Note that α in 1.22 is now the angle of the surface normal with respect to the direction of σ1.


Extending this to 3D we will obtain three principal stresses. By convention they are numbered from the most compressive to the least compressive, i.e., σ1>σ2>σ3. Now obtain Equation 1.19 by balancing a prism in Exercise 1.5.

1.9 Deformation and strain

Here we can think of deformation as a general framework that describes the motion of particles relative to each other in a solid, or relative to a referential. Deformation can be either translation, rotation or strain.

Strain, therefore, is a subset of possible deformation modes. Strain is actually the only deformation mode which produces a distortion of the solid, therefore possibly arising in internal stress. We will see that strain, in the definition adopted here, can be represented as a tensor much alike the stress tensor encountered in the previous sections.


Let’s consider the face of a solid cube represented in figure 1.5. Upon deformation in this case there is apparent strain, as the square is no longer a square in the final state. A particle a inside the face of this cube will be shifted from original position P⁢(xa,ya) to a final position P′⁢(xa′,ya′)=P′⁢(xa+d⁢xa,ya+d⁢ya). The displacement vector, defined at the initial position of the particle, is 𝐮a⁢(xa,ya)=𝐮a⁢(𝐱a)=(xa′-xa,ya′-ya)=(d⁢xa,d⁢ya) Here 𝐱a is a position vector and (d⁢xa,d⁢ya) are the components of the displacement vector 𝐮a. A few things are worth noting here: (1) we may define displacement as final minus initial position (most widespread definition), or initial minus final (this is used for example in the book by Jaeger et al.). Here we opt for the first definition, resulting in negative shrinking and positive expansion. However by convention in Earth Sciences, contrary to Physics, compressive stress is positive by convention, although it corresponds to shrinking. Keep this in mind when dealing with stress in the Earth: we talk about positive compressive quantities, where in fact the actual sign of stress is negative. (2) The displacement vector 𝐮b⁢(𝐱b) is not identical displacement vector 𝐮a⁢(𝐱a), which shows that we do not have a simple translation, but either rotation, or distortion, or both. (3) As a mass particle moves, the referential remains fixed. Displacement is defined with respect to the original position of the particle. We have adopted a Eulerian (fixed) referential rather than a Lagrangian referential (attached to the particle).


Consider now figure 1.6. This is a simpler case where the cube face is only stretched horizontally (along x). If the initial length of the rectangle is ℓ, and the final length is ℓ+Δ⁢ℓ, and if Δ⁢ℓ is small (in the sense that Δ⁢ℓ≪ℓ, which is also called infinitesimal strain), then we can define the strain involved with the stretching as:

Δ⁢ℓℓ+Δ⁢ℓ=Δ⁢ℓℓ⁢(1+Δ⁢ℓℓ)≈Δ⁢ℓℓ (1.29)

The assumption that strain is infinitesimal, importantly, is behind the vast majority of the analysis of elastic defromation, and mostly always the case in seismology. Now take any two points P⁢(xa,ya) and Q⁢(xb,yb) inside the rectangle, displaced by vectors 𝐮a⁢(xa′-xa,0) and 𝐮b⁢(xb′-xb,0), respectively. If we define d⁢x=xb-xa as the x distance between P and Q, and d⁢ux=(xa′-xa)-(xb′-xb) as the displacement difference between P and Q along direction x, we may write

d⁢uxd⁢x=(xa′-xa)-(xb′-xb)xb-xa=d⁢xa-d⁢xbxb-xa. (1.30)

Now provided that the rectangle is streched homogeneously (and if the displacement of paticles is a continuous, harmonic function, we can always choose a rectangle small enough that the variation of the stretching within it is negligible), the we can write:

Δ⁢ℓℓ=d⁢uxd⁢x (1.31)

that is, the relative eleongation of the solid between any two points is the same! Such elongation, in this case is the unidirectional strain along x. In the continuum limit (inifinitesimally small d⁢x) we can write d⁢uxd⁢x=∂⁡ux∂⁡x=∂x⁡ux and define the axial strain as:

εx⁢x=∂⁡ux∂⁡x. (1.32)

Shortening results from compression, corresponding to a positive compressive stress. Elongation results from tension, can corresponds to a negative compressive stress. Underground, in most cases rocks are compressed and compacted by the overburden, whereas streching or tension is a relatively rare situation. Therefore, we implicitly assume that we are talking about compressive stress, and use positive values. In Physics, the opposite convention is adopted: positive stress values correspond to streching, and negative values correspond to compression. In the same way as εx⁢x, one can define two additional elongation strains in three dimensions, namely εy⁢y=∂y⁡uy and εz⁢z=∂z⁡uz. By analogy, shear strain can be also defined (for example if the displacement ux is varying along direction y). For all possible combinations of displacement and direction, we may in principle define an additional set of shear strains such as εx⁢y,εy⁢x,εx⁢z,εz⁢x,εy⁢z,εz⁢y. However, in the case of shear need to separate the rigid rotation from the actual strain. Consider the rigid rotation of figure 1.7.


and assuming that θ≪1 we can write the x2+y2≈x′⁣2+y′⁣2=r, therefore the displacement P-P′ is

d⁢x=r⁢(cos⁡θ-cos⁡θ′)d⁢y=r⁢(sin⁡θ-sin⁡θ′), (1.33)

Consider now the deformation with respect to point O⁢(0,0), the cross-terms will be

d⁢uxy=(cos⁡θ-cos⁡θ′)sin⁡θd⁢uyx=(sin⁡θ-sin⁡θ′)cos⁡θ (1.34)

and we clearly see that each deformation term alone is non-zero, despite it being pure rotation and no strain. Wanting to define strain, we need a formulation which somehow zeroes the rotation. To this purpose, we may notice that the two cross-term have the opposite value in the case of rigid rotation , i.e.,

d⁢uxy=-d⁢uyx  (upon rigid rotation) (1.35)

(and to demonstrate it, we can solve Exercise 1.6). In fact the equality 1.35 regarding point P and the rotation center O, also works for any two points P and Q in the same neighbourhood. To demonstrate why, see Exercise 1.7. Therefore we can generalise that between any two neighbouring points P and Q, the sum of the cross-deformation terms will cancel the contribution due to solid rotation, such that ∂⁡uxd⁢x|rot.=-∂⁡uyd⁢y|rot.. We then define:

εx⁢y=12⁢(∂⁡ux∂⁡y+∂⁡uy∂⁡x) (1.36)

which zeroes the contribution of rigid rotation, and note that it is symmetrical:

εx⁢y=εy⁢x, (1.37)

in the same way as the stress tensor is. As a consequence in three dimensions we only need half the cross-terms to define shear (three terms), and three compressive terms, and we can organise them as a tensor which has the same symmetry as the stress tensor:

𝜺=(εx⁢xεx⁢yεx⁢z(εy⁢x)εy⁢yεy⁢z(εz⁢x)(εz⁢y)εz⁢z) (1.38)

(the three terms in parethesis are redundant because of symmetry). In contracted notation:

εi⁢j=12(∂⁡ui∂⁡xj+∂⁡uj∂⁡xi)=12(∂jui+∂iuj) (1.39)

where i,j,k can be replaced as needed by any of x,y or z.

Answer to Exercise 1.4 [ – Tangential stress balance] We are going to balance the moments due to the shear tractions on the 4 faces of the square, with respect to the center of the square. Let the side of the square be of length 2⁢a. The shear stress on right side and left side of the square sides is τx⁢y, on the top and bottom it is τy⁢x (first index indicates the face normal, second index direction of stress).

Let us look at the right-hand face, and at point P. Across a small surface d⁢z×d⁢y of the face around point P, the stress will induce a force d⁢f=d⁢z⁢d⁢y⁢τx⁢y, but we work in 2D so we can assume d⁢z=1 and d⁢f=d⁢y⁢τx⁢y. The force d⁢f will tend to rotate the square clockwise, by acting on the arm OP (line going from the barycenter of the square at point O to point P). The length of arm OP is a/cos⁡θ. We need to project d⁢f onto the perpendicular to the arm obtaining

d⁢f⁢cos⁡θ=d⁢y⁢τx⁢y⁢cos⁡θ

and then multiply it by the arm length O⁢P=a/cos⁡θ, to obtain the net moment d⁢m around point P such that:

d⁢m=-cos⁡θ⁢d⁢y⁢τx⁢y×acos⁡θ=-d⁢y⁢τx⁢y⁢a.

(there is a minus sign because we choose, as of convention, anti-clockwise rotation as positive, clockwise as negative). Now to obtain the net moment m1 across the right-hand side face, we should integrate d⁢m over the entire face, with y from -a to +a, namely:

m1=-a⁢∫-aaτx⁢y⁢𝑑y=-2⁢a2⁢τx⁢y.

Proceeding likewise, we obtain the exact same result for the moment on the left hand side:

m2=-2⁢a2⁢τx⁢y

and for the top and bottom (here with sign +, as the rotation impulse is anti-clockwise):

m3=2⁢a2⁢τy⁢x
m4=2⁢a2⁢τy⁢x.

Summing all the moments should result in zero net momentum, so that the cube would not rotate:

m1+m2+m3+m4 = 0
2a2(-2τx⁢y +2τy⁢x)= 0
τx⁢y = τy⁢x

Answer to Exercise 1.5

We start by resolving all stress contributions in σ-parallel direction, and multiply each by the area of the face it is applied to:

AB⁢σ =OA⁢cos⁡θ⁢σx⁢x+OB⁢sin⁡θ⁢σy⁢y
+OB⁢τy⁢x⁢cos⁡θ+OA⁢τx⁢y⁢sin⁡θ.

Then we divide all terms by AB and note that the length ratios equate to

OAAB =cos⁡θ⁢ and ⁢OBAB=sin⁡θ

to obtain:

σ =σx⁢x⁢cos2⁡θ+σy⁢y⁢sin2⁡θ
+2⁢τx⁢y⁢sin⁡θ⁢cos⁡θ.

We do the same for the τ-parallel components:

AB⁢τ= -OA⁢sin⁡θ⁢σx⁢x+OB⁢cos⁡θ⁢σy⁢y
+OA⁢τx⁢y⁢cos⁡θ-OB⁢τy⁢x⁢sin⁡θ
τ= (cos⁡θ⁢sin⁡θ)⁢(σy⁢y-σx⁢x)
+τx⁢y⁢(cos2⁡θ-sin2⁡θ)

Finally, noting that:

cos2⁡θ-sin2⁡θ=cos⁡2⁢θ    and that    cos2⁡θ+sin2⁡θ=1

we can write:

cos2⁡θ=sin2⁡θ+cos⁡2⁢θcos2⁡θ=1-cos2⁡θ+cos⁡2⁢θ2⁢cos2⁡θ=1+cos⁡2⁢θcos2⁡θ=1/2⁢(1+cos⁡2⁢θ)

and

sin2⁡θ=cos2⁡θ-cos⁡2⁢θsin2⁡θ=1-sin2⁡θ-cos⁡2⁢θ2⁢sin2⁡θ=1-cos⁡2⁢θsin2⁡θ=1/2⁢(1-cos⁡2⁢θ)

and noting that:

sin⁡θ⁢cos⁡θ=1/2⁢sin⁡2⁢θ

we can write:

σ=12⁢(σx⁢x+σy⁢y)+12⁢(σx⁢x-σy⁢y)⁢cos⁡2⁢θ+τx⁢y⁢sin⁡2⁢θ
τ=12⁢(σy⁢y-σx⁢x)⁢sin⁡2⁢θ+τx⁢y⁢cos⁡2⁢θ

Answer to Exercise 1.7 The sum of cross-terms is zero between any point and the rotation center O. Therefore it is zero for an arbitrary point P with respect to O, and also for an arbitrary point Q with respect to O. Now a solid rotation generates a displacement which is continuous and harmonic, and its gradient is also continuous and harmonic. Strain is the sum of displacement gradients. Therefore the strain from P to Q is equal to the strain from P to O plus the strain from O to Q, and if each is zero the sum of the two is also zero.
